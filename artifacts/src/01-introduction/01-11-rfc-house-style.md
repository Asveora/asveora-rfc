## 1.11.1 RFC House Style

The Asveora RFC is written as a constitutional protocol specification intended to define the architectural, operational, and conceptual foundations of the Asveora Ecosystem. It exists simultaneously as a deterministic technical reference, a systems architecture document, and an educational framework for understanding decentralized identity and federation systems. Accordingly, the RFC is not structured as a marketing document, promotional whitepaper, abstract academic paper, or implementation-specific API reference. Its purpose is to define the canonical rules, structures, relationships, and behaviors that govern interoperable participation within the Ecosystem while remaining understandable to readers across multiple technical backgrounds and disciplines.

The writing philosophy of this RFC prioritizes conceptual fidelity over artificial simplification. The objective is not to remove complexity from distributed systems, identity architectures, federation coordination, or governance semantics, but rather to organize such complexity into structures that remain coherent, navigable, and understandable without sacrificing architectural precision. Asveora intentionally rejects excessive abstraction, recursive metaphor layering, and terminology drift in favor of direct system descriptions rooted in deterministic language and canonical definitions. While explanatory analogies MAY occasionally be used for onboarding purposes, they are supplementary in nature and MUST NOT replace formally defined terminology or protocol semantics.

## 1.11.2 Documentation Philosophy

Terminology within this RFC is treated as a structural component of the architecture itself rather than as interchangeable descriptive language. Canonical terms define specific conceptual entities and therefore MUST remain internally consistent throughout the specification. Once a term has been formally defined, future sections are expected to inherit and reference that definition rather than reinterpret, restate, or recursively recontextualize it. This approach exists to preserve long-term interpretive stability, reduce ambiguity between implementations, and prevent conceptual fragmentation across future revisions of the RFC. The specification therefore operates as a progressively layered document in which later sections build directly upon terminology and relationships established in earlier foundational sections.

The RFC is additionally structured around a principle of progressive conceptual accumulation. Readers are expected to develop understanding incrementally as concepts are introduced, constrained, and operationalized across the document. Earlier sections establish constitutional intent, foundational terminology, and architectural relationships while later sections define operational behavior, interoperability semantics, lifecycle states, federation coordination, validation systems, and implementation expectations. As such, concepts that have already been canonically established SHOULD NOT be repeatedly re-explained in later sections unless clarification is required to resolve ambiguity, define exceptions, or describe materially distinct operational behavior.

The rhetorical structure of this RFC intentionally separates constitutional philosophy, architectural composition, and operational behavior into distinct conceptual layers even when those layers remain closely interconnected. Constitutional sections define foundational principles and ecosystem intent. Architectural sections define structural relationships between systems and protocol primitives. Operational sections define deterministic runtime behavior, synchronization requirements, lifecycle expectations, and interoperability semantics. This separation exists to ensure that philosophical reasoning, structural composition, and implementation behavior remain distinguishable from one another while still operating as part of a coherent unified specification.

Paragraphs within this RFC are expected to maintain disciplined conceptual scope. A paragraph SHOULD primarily perform a single rhetorical function such as defining a concept, explaining a relationship, constraining interpretation, contextualizing behavior, or describing operational consequences. Excessive layering of multiple rhetorical purposes within a single paragraph SHOULD be avoided where possible in order to preserve readability, pacing, and conceptual clarity. Similarly, parenthetical clarification SHOULD remain limited to brief precision notes, acronym expansion, or narrowly scoped contextual qualifiers. Explanations requiring substantial elaboration SHOULD instead be expressed as independent sentences or dedicated subsections.

## 1.11.3 Contributor Guidelines and Recommendations

The RFC favors continuous prose and compositional explanation over excessive fragmentation into isolated bullet lists or disconnected sentence fragments. Lists MAY still be employed where hierarchy, ordering, dependency relationships, or reference utility materially benefit the reader, but the overall documentation philosophy prioritizes narrative continuity and architectural flow. This approach reflects the understanding that federation systems, identity models, governance semantics, and distributed interoperability mechanisms are deeply relational concepts that become increasingly difficult to understand when excessively fragmented across disconnected structural references.

Normative language within this RFC is reserved for enforceable protocol behavior and interoperability requirements. Terms such as MUST, SHOULD, MAY, SHALL, and related requirement-level language are intended to carry deterministic operational significance consistent with RFC 2119 conventions. Normative terminology SHOULD therefore be used precisely and intentionally rather than rhetorically or conversationally. Non-normative commentary, explanatory guidance, onboarding clarifications, and illustrative examples MAY accompany normative definitions but MUST NOT override canonical protocol behavior or alter deterministic interpretation.

Descriptions of mechanics may be compressed, authority origin (who defines it), resolution path (who reads it), and inheritance rule (who depends on it) MUST be preserved. Each major section that defines a core model (like Identity Model, Federation Model, Registry Model, etc.) ends with a single optional closure subsection that follows a consistent structure. This is known as the Structural Stability Pattern (SSP) and has the following format demonstrated as an example:

**X.Y.Z Structural Stability**

This section is considered structurally stable for downstream reference. Its definitions MAY be referenced in later sections without restatement. Any ambiguity or conflict in interpretation MUST defer to this section unless explicitly revised through a versioned protocol update. No additional commentary, examples, or expansion is included here.

The intended reading experience of this RFC is that of a technically literate systems architect directly explaining the structure and behavior of a decentralized federation ecosystem without obscuring complexity behind abstraction, excessive simplification, or implementation-specific assumptions. Readers are expected to engage actively with the material, reference canonical terminology when necessary, and progressively build understanding through the layered structure of the specification. The RFC therefore assumes reader attentiveness and conceptual continuity rather than continuously resetting context for each newly introduced section.

Finally, this documentation philosophy exists to preserve long-term architectural stability across the evolution of the Asveora Ecosystem. The protocol is intended to support heterogeneous implementations, sovereign federation environments, portable identity continuity, and interoperable distributed systems over extended periods of time. Achieving those goals requires that terminology remain stable, architectural intent remain coherent, protocol semantics remain deterministic, and conceptual definitions remain resistant to abstraction drift across future revisions. Accordingly, all future sections and amendments to this RFC SHOULD adhere to the writing principles, interpretive constraints, and structural conventions established herein unless explicitly superseded by a higher-order constitutional or versioned protocol revision.
