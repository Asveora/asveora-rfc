## 1.6.1 Intended Technical Depth

This document is written at a systems and protocol specification level which means that concepts are defined in abstract, implementation-agnostic terms. Behavior is described in deterministic, normative language while architecture is expressed in compositional components and relationships. Application-layer behavior is intentionally excluded unless necessary for protocol clarity. Readers should expect content similar in nature to federation protocol specifications, identity system RFCs, distributed registry models, or authentication and trust system glossaries.

## 1.6.2 Conceptual Familiarity, Terminology, and Structure

Throughout this RFC, readers will be made familiar with concepts such as distributed systems architecture, identity and authentication models, federated network topologies, client-server and multi-instance systems, data consistency and synchronization concepts, and basic cryptographic identity principles (conceptual, not implementation-level). While deep cryptographic engineering knowledge is not required, readers should be comfortable reasoning about identity verification, trust relationships, and system interoperability. If you are not comfortable with reasoning about those concepts, do not panic! This RFC will serve as a good entry point into becoming familiar with those items. However, we do encourage that you fill in any knowledge gaps with supplemental reading about those topics if you still feel confused or unsure about certain concepts. Asveora always promotes the love of learning!

This RFC introduces a high density of domain-specific terminology, including but not limited to **PIK (Primary Identity Key)**, **VSIG (Verification Signature)**, **APA (Asveora Profile Address)**, **AUID (Asveora Unified Identity Descriptor)**, **CIR (Canonical Identity Record)**, **Wheelcore**, **Network**, federation synchronization models, and registry validation systems. Readers are expected to refer to the Canonical Terminology Appendix when encountering unfamiliar terms.

The RFC is structured as a layered specification which means later sections depend on definitions established in earlier sections. This is why reading it in order on your first read through is so important. Identity, federation, and governance systems are interdependent while operational models assume understanding of prior architectural primitives. Reading sections out of order may result in incomplete interpretation and we want to avoid that at all costs! Therefore, as stated before, readers are strongly encouraged to read the document sequentially.

Please keep in mind that the specification operates at a single depth of abstraction to remain implementation-agnostic, support multiple independent deployments, allow heterogeneous runtime systems, and preserve long-term protocol stability. As a result, there is no specific programming language assumed, no database schema mandated, no UI or frontend behavior defined, and no infrastructure stack of a particular kind designated. The expectation is for implementers to translate the protocol definitions presented within this RFC into concrete system designs.
