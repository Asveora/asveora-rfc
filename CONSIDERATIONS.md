### AmzroSevca's RFC Development Notes

The biggest recurring risk in the Asveora RFC is people mistaking declarative structure for executable behavior. Almost every major system artifact visually resembles something operational, even when it is not intended to function that way. Schemas look like runtime definitions, validation pipelines look like execution engines, state machines look like orchestration logic, and layering diagrams look like runtime stack order. The RFC repeatedly defines validity, consistency, relationships, and constraints rather than prescribing implementation behavior, but the terminology naturally pushes readers toward runtime assumptions anyway. The safest mental model is that the RFC describes what is structurally valid and semantically consistent, not what executes, who executes it, or how execution must occur.

The CIR concept is especially vulnerable to semantic overload. It is easy for readers to unconsciously treat CIR as a database object, authority system, runtime identity object, or network entity simply because it occupies a central role in the architecture. The distinction that needs constant reinforcement is that CIR represents canonical identity structure, not the systems that store, transport, synchronize, or enforce identity state. Registries, federation payloads, governance records, and authorization layers all interact with CIR, but none of them replace or redefine it. The moment these boundaries blur, the conceptual architecture starts collapsing into implementation assumptions.

The validation and determinism language also requires careful handling because both terms carry heavy distributed systems implications. Validation pipelines naturally resemble workflow engines or middleware chains, even though they are intended as ordered inspection and consistency checkpoints rather than runtime processing systems. Determinism introduces another layer of risk because readers may interpret it as a promise of globally deterministic distributed execution when the actual intent is closer to deterministic interpretation and structural evaluation. In practice, the RFC appears concerned with deterministic understanding of system state rather than deterministic runtime behavior under distributed load.

There is also a recurring pattern where observational or evaluative systems appear more authoritative than they actually are. Governance schemas can easily look like enforcement engines because they contain policy oriented terminology. Authorization layers can feel like identity controllers because RBAC systems traditionally imply operational control. State machines can appear to govern execution because they define allowed transitions. In each case, the RFC seems to describe permitted structures, valid transitions, or auditable events rather than the systems responsible for enforcing those outcomes. The distinction between defining constraints and executing authority is subtle, but it may be one of the most important conceptual boundaries in the entire architecture.

Federation introduces another high risk area because distributed systems vocabulary tends to imply consensus and authority sharing even when that is not intended. Readers may assume federation performs reconciliation, conflict resolution, or authoritative synchronization simply because those behaviors are commonly associated with federated systems. The current framing appears narrower and cleaner than that. Federation functions more as transport and interoperability infrastructure rather than truth arbitration. Sync operations move representations of state rather than determining the truth of state itself. Keeping that separation explicit is probably critical to preventing future architectural confusion.

One of the deeper long term risks is over unification. There is a strong temptation in large architectures to make every schema executable, every abstraction operational, and every layer directly implemented. The RFC does not seem designed that way. Some structures exist primarily to constrain interpretation, preserve semantic consistency, or create safe boundaries for future implementations. If future contributors begin forcing every conceptual layer into runtime systems, the architecture could become unnecessarily rigid and over engineered. Some parts of the RFC appear intentionally descriptive rather than operational, and preserving that distinction may be necessary for the system to remain flexible over time.

The proposed Union abstraction is potentially useful, but it also introduces significant semantic danger because it broadens the hierarchy into a generalized collective model. The idea itself is coherent since both Systems and Federations are aggregations of smaller autonomous entities, but the abstraction risks collapsing important distinctions between structural grouping, governance, coordination, and authority. Once Union becomes an umbrella term, readers may start asking whether it is a runtime entity, a governance class, a semantic grouping, or a functional network layer. If those answers are not extremely precise, the abstraction could weaken some of the RFC’s existing conceptual clarity. The concern that Union could accidentally break the RFC is probably valid because it introduces another layer where structural and operational assumptions can become entangled.

A large portion of the overall challenge comes from terminology itself. Words like commit, migration, federation, authority, transition, synchronization, validation, and governance all carry industry expectations that people automatically import into their interpretation of the RFC. Even when the architecture is technically precise, readers may still project implementation assumptions onto concepts simply because the vocabulary feels operational. This means wording discipline is not cosmetic in the RFC. It is structural. The architecture depends heavily on maintaining clean separation between descriptive semantics and executable behavior.

The most stable mental model still seems to be the simplest one. The Asveora RFC defines structural semantics, consistency rules, and identity interpretation boundaries for distributed identity ecosystems. It is not itself a runtime architecture, orchestration engine, governance authority, database specification, or distributed execution framework. Many parts of the RFC exist to prevent invalid interpretations rather than to directly execute behavior, and remembering that consistently may be the key to keeping the architecture coherent as it evolves.
